Neural state space diagrams can, in principle, have as many axes as there are recorded neurons. In practice, neural activity typically varies along a much smaller set of directions—dimensions (Cueva et al., 2020; Stokes et al., 2015; Cowley et al., 2016; Haxby et al., 2001). Because neurons are correlated, each one does not contribute independently to the population signal (Nienborg et al., 2012; Schölvinck et al., 2015; Averbeck et al., 2006; Rabinowitz et al., 2015; Adibi et al., 2013); instead, combinations of neurons account for most of the variability (Ponce‐Alvarez et al., 2013; Cohen et al., 2009). This redundancy allows us to apply dimensionality reduction to project the original, neuron-dimensional space onto a smaller set of axes. One common choice for these axes is principal component analysis (PCA).
