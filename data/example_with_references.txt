Neural state space diagrams can, in principle, have as many axes as there are recorded neurons. In practice, neural activity typically varies along a much smaller set of directionsâ€”dimensions (Barratt et al., 1996; Hemmateenejad et al., 2003; Vendrame et al., 1999; Friman et al., 2001; Friman et al., 2001). Because neurons are correlated, each one does not contribute independently to the population signal (Rabinowitz et al., 2015; Cohen et al., 2009; Panzeri et al., 2015; Canady et al., 1994; Cayco-Gajic et al., 2014); instead, combinations of neurons account for most of the variability (Lee et al., 1998; Mancia et al., 2013; Henseler et al., 2009; Salinas et al., 2000; Rabinowitz et al., 2015). This redundancy allows us to apply dimensionality reduction to project the original, neuron-dimensional space onto a smaller set of axes. One common choice for these axes is principal component analysis (PCA).
